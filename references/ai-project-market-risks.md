# AI Project Market Risks

Use this reference when the project is AI-native, AI-assisted, agentic, automated, or sold primarily as an AI product. The purpose is to separate real market demand from AI-assisted wishful thinking.

## Core Test

Ask:

> If the user could not mention AI, would the target customer still understand and value the delivered result?

If the answer is no, the project may be demo novelty rather than market demand.

## Risk Areas

### 1. AI-Backward Reasoning

Red flags:

- "AI can automate this" is treated as proof that customers need it.
- The builder starts from model capability instead of customer pain.
- The pitch emphasizes the AI mechanism more than the delivered outcome.
- No one currently spends time, money, staff, or attention on the problem.

Market check:

- What painful workflow existed before AI entered the sentence?
- What current alternative proves the problem matters?
- What cost does the customer pay today by not solving it?

### 2. Trust and Error Cost

Red flags:

- AI errors could create financial, legal, safety, reputation, or customer-trust damage.
- The user cannot easily verify the AI output.
- The product asks users to delegate judgment before trust exists.

Market check:

- What level of accuracy is good enough?
- Who reviews or approves the output?
- What happens when the AI is wrong?
- Can the product start as a copilot, audit, draft, or recommendation instead of full automation?

### 3. Data and Permission Friction

Red flags:

- The product needs sensitive data before proving value.
- Security, privacy, compliance, or procurement blocks adoption.
- The user must connect many tools before first value.

Market check:

- Can first value be shown with sample, synthetic, public, or manually provided data?
- What is the minimum data needed to validate demand?
- Who has permission to provide the data?

### 4. Adoption and Workflow Fit

Red flags:

- The AI output is interesting but does not fit an existing workflow.
- The product creates more review work than it saves.
- The buyer is not the daily user.
- The user would try it once but has no repeated trigger.

Market check:

- What recurring event causes the user to need this?
- Where does the output go after generation?
- What behavior must change for adoption?
- What existing workflow does it replace, shorten, or improve?

### 5. Cheap Human or Existing Tool Alternative

Red flags:

- A spreadsheet, freelancer, template, assistant, or current SaaS tool is good enough.
- The AI version is faster but not meaningfully better.
- The customer does not experience the current workaround as painful.

Market check:

- Is the AI cheaper, faster, safer, higher quality, or more scalable?
- Is that improvement large enough to cause switching?
- What proof would make the user stop using the current alternative?

### 6. One-Time Novelty

Red flags:

- Users say "cool" but do not return.
- The product produces a wow demo but no repeated job.
- Usage depends on curiosity rather than a recurring trigger.

Market check:

- What would make the user come back weekly or monthly?
- What business or personal consequence does repeated use affect?
- What is the retention event?

## Verdict Guidance

- **Build** only when there is direct evidence of demand, a clear delivery object, reachable users, and manageable trust/adoption risk.
- **Validate** when demand may exist but the buyer, urgency, willingness to pay, or adoption path is still assumed.
- **Hold** when the project is mostly AI capability, vague audience, weak pain, unclear delivery, or no credible path to evidence.

The skill must be allowed to say:

> Hold. Do not build yet. Validate the demand before designing the product.
